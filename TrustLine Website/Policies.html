Security Policies

Policies Every SME Should Have

Most security incidents happen because there were no clear rules, or the rules existed but nobody actually knew about them. These aren't meant to be perfect documents. They're a starting point, and having something in place is genuinely better than nothing.

6 policies ~2 hours to draft No legal jargon
Not sure where to start? Go with the Information Security Policy first, it sets the tone for everything else. From there, work down the list at whatever pace suits you. You don't need to do them all at once.

Information Security Policy

This is your top-level document. It covers how your organisation handles security overall, not in fine detail, just the key commitments. It doesn't need to be long, but it should be clear about who's responsible for what and why it matters.

Protecting customer and employee data Confidentiality, integrity and availability Who is responsible for security decisions Acceptable use of company systems How often the policy gets reviewed

Password and Access Control Policy

One of the more straightforward wins. Getting access control right prevents a lot of problems before they start. And removing access when someone leaves? Just as important as setting it up properly in the first place.

Minimum password requirements for all staff MFA on all accounts where possible Access based on role, nothing extra Revoking access the same day someone leaves

Email and Phishing Awareness Policy

Phishing is still the most common way businesses get compromised, by a significant margin. This policy gives staff something concrete to refer to, not just "be careful" and hope for the best.

  • How to spot suspicious links and attachments
  • Not sharing passwords over email or chat
  • Where to report it, and what to do if you think you clicked something

Acceptable Use Policy

Covers what staff can and can't do with company devices and accounts. Less useful as a disciplinary tool, more useful as a way of making expectations clear before anything goes wrong.

Internet and email usage on work devices Installing software without IT approval Using personal devices for work (BYOD) What can and cannot be shared externally Using AI tools or third-party apps for work tasks

Data Protection and Privacy Policy

If you collect or store personal data, you need this one. It covers how data should be handled, how long to keep it, and what to do if something goes wrong.

Particularly relevant if you're subject to UK GDPR, which applies to most UK businesses whether they realise it or not.

Lawful basis for collecting personal data Retention periods and deletion procedures Who can access personal data internally Steps to take if a breach is suspected

Incident Response Policy

When something goes wrong, people need to know what to do rather than work it out under pressure. Even a basic one-page plan helps. It doesn't have to cover every scenario, just enough to stop people freezing up and making things worse.

What counts as a security incident worth reporting Who to contact and within what timeframe Immediate steps to contain the problem Logging what happened and reviewing it afterwards
